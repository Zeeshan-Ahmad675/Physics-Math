1. Basic Geometry

  • Pythagoras Theorem:

    • For a right angled triangle, as shown in figure, with the hypotenuse \(c\), base \(b\), and height \(a\): $$c^2=a^2+b^2 \tag{1.1}$$ This relation was given by ancient Greek philosopher, Pythagoras upon whom the theorem is named.
    This thorem would be used for finding most prominantly the length of vectors, followed by its traditional applications in finding the side length of right-angled triangles and much more.

    Here is a simple proof using triangle similarity.

    • Proof:

      • Consider the figure again for \(\triangle BCD\) and \(\triangle ABC\). Clearly, $$\angle CBD = \angle CBA =\theta$$ $$\angle BCD = \angle BAC =90^{\circ}-\theta$$ $$\angle BDC = \angle BCA =90^{\circ}$$ This shows that the \(\triangle BCD \sim \triangle ABC, \) by the \(AAA\) similarity criterion. A similar argument for \(\triangle ACD\) and \(\triangle ABC\) would give \(\triangle ACD \sim \triangle ABC. \)

      Using similarity, $$\frac{BC}{BD}=\frac{AB}{BC}\implies BD= \frac{BC^2}{AB}\tag{i}$$ and $$\frac{AB}{AC}=\frac{AC}{AD}\implies AD= \frac{AC^2}{AB}\tag{ii}$$ Using,\((\)i\()\) and \((\)ii\()\) and using the fact that \(AD+BD=AB\), we get:

      $$AB=AD+BD = \frac{BC^2}{AB} + \frac{AC^2}{AB} $$ $$\implies AB^2= BC^2+AC^2 $$ $$\implies \boxed{c^2=a^2+b^2}$$
      ,which proves the theorem.
